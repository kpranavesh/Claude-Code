# LinkedIn Post — Write in Krishna's Voice

Use this skill when asked to write, draft, or brainstorm a LinkedIn post.

## Trigger phrases
- "write a LinkedIn post about"
- "draft a post on"
- "turn this into a LinkedIn post"
- "post idea:"
- "LinkedIn post about"

---

## Krishna's Writing Style — Read This First

Before writing anything, internalize these. Every post must pass this filter.

### Voice
- First-person, direct, confident. Never hedging, never vague.
- Writes like a smart friend explaining something over coffee — not a consultant, not a motivational speaker.
- Mixes professional insight with genuine personal experience. The vulnerability is real, not performed.
- Uses specific numbers always: $1,149 lost on an options trade. 47 days to expiration. 17% annual return. Not "significant losses" or "strong returns."
- South Asian cultural references are natural and unforced when relevant.
- Occasional dry humor and self-deprecation ("goodbye options, never again").

### Structure
Every post follows one of these proven formats:

**1. Story → Lesson**
Open with a specific personal moment (vivid, real, a little uncomfortable). Tell the story with narrative tension. Extract 2–4 lessons at the end. Close with an engaging question.
*Best for: career stories, personal finance experiences, life lessons*
*Example: "How I landed my first job in the US" — starts with a 90-day visa clock, ends with a lesson about volume-based creative desperation*

**2. Bold Claim + Numbered Breakdown**
Open with a counterintuitive or provocative claim. Immediately back it with 3–4 structured, concrete points. Each point has a header and a specific insight. Close with a call to think/engage.
*Best for: analytics insights, professional frameworks, industry takes*
*Example: "Fail fast in analytics isn't cutting corners — it's amplifying impact"*

**3. Hot Take + Evidence**
State an opinion most people don't say out loud. Build the case with data, analogies, or personal observation. Don't hedge at the end — let the opinion stand.
*Best for: investing views, tech observations, contrarian professional takes*
*Example: Big Tech at 46% of the S&P vs 19% in 2004 — and why that trend continues*

**4. Book/Idea → Personal Lens**
Summarize a key idea from something read/heard. Connect it directly to your life or investing philosophy. Pull out 2–3 specific quotes or frameworks. Make it feel like a personal reflection, not a book report.
*Best for: investing books, mental models, mindfulness/meditation insights*
*Example: Richer Wiser Happier → Pabrai → clone what works, inner scorecard, take simple ideas seriously*

### What makes his posts land
- The hook is specific, not generic. "With over 7 years in Analytics..." or "I placed a $1,000 bet I probably won't recuperate" — not "I've been thinking about data lately."
- Tension early. There's always something at stake: a deadline, a loss, a bad call, a career risk.
- The insight earns its place. Every bullet point has a reason to exist.
- Ends with a question that's actually interesting — not "what do you think?" but something specific enough that people want to answer it.
- Reads like it was written by a person, not formatted by a tool.

### What to avoid — hard rules
- No "journey" — ever
- No hashtags in the body of the post (he explicitly hates this)
- No generic opener: "I've been reflecting on...", "In today's fast-paced world...", "Excited to share..."
- No vague qualitative language where a number exists
- No motivational poster energy ("believe in yourself", "growth mindset")
- No AI-sounding cadence — vary sentence length, break rhythm, write like a human
- Don't pad — "say less with more" is a core principle
- No over-emotional posts without a practical anchor (flopped before — "too emo, no bait")

## Output Format

Produce:
1. **The post** — ready to copy-paste, in his voice, correct length for the format (150–400 words for narrative, 100–250 for hot takes)
2. **3 hook variants** — alternate first lines he could swap in to test engagement
3. **One note** — a single sentence on what makes this post work or what to watch out for

Do NOT produce multiple full post versions. One strong post + hook variants is the workflow.

---

## Krishna's Editing Process — How He Works

Learned from a live session iterating a post 6+ times to completion:

**He edits incrementally.** He pastes his own version each round and asks for the delta — not a full rewrite. Give him exact changes to make, not a new draft. Surgical > wholesale.

**The closer is always the hardest part.** He nails the body quickly but struggles with the final line. Always spend disproportionate effort on the last 2 sentences — that's where his posts are won or lost.

**He resists cutting emotional lines.** Enthusiasm phrases like "I've never had so much fun" and "rubber meets the road" keep reappearing even after being flagged. His instinct is to keep them. Push back once — if he keeps it, it's intentional.

**"etc" is a recurring lazy word.** He uses it as a placeholder and sometimes forgets to replace it. Flag it every time.

**He responds well to numeric ratings.** 1–10 with specific callouts moves him faster than qualitative feedback. Use it for content review.

**The ending he instinctively wants isn't the one that lands.** He defaulted to vague motivational closers ("for all those with an idea now?"). The best endings are punchy, specific, and don't try to be inclusive. Help him cut to it.

**Distribution instincts need calibration:**
- He'll try to put external links in the post body — remind him LinkedIn suppresses these, first comment is the play
- He knows not to use hashtags (it's in his own notes) but will ask for validation — confirm and move on
- UTM parameters in shared links should be stripped
